# I Thought I Was “Too Much” Until I Understood Trauma

For most of my life, I genuinely believed there was something fundamentally wrong with me.

Too emotional.
Too sensitive.
Too intense.
Too reactive.
Too attached.
Too overwhelmed.
Too affected by things other people seemed able to handle.

I spent years trying to become “less.”

Less emotional.
Less frightened.
Less affected.
Less expressive.
Less needy.
Less human.

Because when you grow up around emotional unsafety, you often learn very quickly that your feelings are inconvenient to other people.

So you begin shrinking yourself to survive.

Masking.
Apologising.
Overexplaining.
Monitoring your reactions constantly.
Trying desperately to appear “reasonable” enough to remain emotionally safe around others.

I became very skilled at hiding distress.

But hidden distress does not disappear.

It settles into the nervous system instead.

That is the part I understand now.

Trauma is not simply remembering painful things.

Trauma changes the way the body experiences the world.

It changes how quickly you enter survival mode.
How deeply you fear misunderstanding.
How intensely your body reacts to conflict, rejection, unpredictability or emotional distance.

Especially when autism already means you process emotion, sensory information and social dynamics intensely.

For years I thought I was failing at being a normal human being.

I now realise my nervous system was carrying far more fear than most people could actually see.

And once I understood that, something inside me softened.

Not completely.

But enough to stop viewing myself purely through shame.

I think many traumatised people are walking around believing they are “too much” when in reality they are humans whose nervous systems adapted to prolonged emotional unsafety the only way they knew how.

Not broken.

Adapted.

There is a difference.
