# The Body Keeps the Score of Everything the File Missed

Nobody saw how frightened my body was because I still looked functional.

I could still speak properly.
Still attend meetings.
Still answer questions.
Still smile sometimes.
Still get the children dressed for school.
Still make dinner.
Still survive.

So people assumed I was coping.

But trauma does not always look dramatic.

Sometimes it looks like exhaustion so deep you cannot explain it.
Sometimes it looks like overexplaining because your body is terrified of being misunderstood.
Sometimes it looks like hypervigilance so intense your nervous system never fully switches off.
Sometimes it looks like appearing “too emotional” when your body has spent years living like danger could happen at any second.

I am autistic, and I did not understand for a long time how deeply prolonged fear lives inside the nervous system.

People saw behaviour.
They did not always see survival.

People saw reactions.
They did not always see terror.

People saw intensity.
They did not always see a human being trying desperately to stay safe while still appearing functional to the outside world.

That is one of the hardest parts to explain about trauma.

The body remembers things the mouth cannot always put into words.

It remembers fear.
It remembers unpredictability.
It remembers walking on eggshells.
It remembers never fully relaxing.
It remembers surviving for so long that survival mode starts feeling like your personality.

For years I believed I was simply “too sensitive.”

Now I understand my nervous system had been carrying prolonged fear for far too long.

I think more professionals need to understand the difference between distress and danger.

Because sometimes a frightened nervous system can look chaotic on the outside while the person underneath is simply exhausted from surviving.

Files record behaviour.

But they do not always record the emotional reality of being human.
