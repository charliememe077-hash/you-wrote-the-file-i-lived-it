# The Day I Realised I Was Always Bracing

I did not even notice I was doing it at first.

That is how normal survival mode had become inside my body.

Until one day I realised:
I was constantly bracing for something.

A message.
A tone change.
Bad news.
Conflict.
Misunderstanding.
Rejection.
Something going wrong.

Even during ordinary moments.

Even in silence.

Even while making coffee.
Watching television.
Folding washing.
Sitting with the children.

Part of my nervous system was always waiting.

Always preparing emotionally for impact.

That is what prolonged fear can do to the body.

It teaches you that safety is temporary.

So your nervous system stops fully relaxing because it believes staying alert might protect you from pain.

I think many traumatised people become so used to internal tension that they no longer even recognise it.

Hypervigilance starts feeling normal.

Bracing starts feeling normal.

Fear starts feeling normal.

Until one small moment of calm suddenly reveals how tightly your body has been holding itself together for years.

That realisation is heartbreaking.

Because you begin to understand how exhausted your nervous system actually is.

Not from weakness.

From survival.

From carrying emotional alertness for too long.

People often think trauma lives in memories.

But sometimes trauma lives in the body as anticipation.

The constant expectation that something painful, frightening or emotionally unsafe is about to happen.

Even when nothing bad is happening anymore.

I think some nervous systems become trapped between past danger and future fear.

Never fully arriving in the present moment because the body learned too early that peace could disappear at any time.

And after enough years of living like that, rest itself can start feeling unfamiliar.

Not because you do not want peace.

Because your body forgot how to trust it.
