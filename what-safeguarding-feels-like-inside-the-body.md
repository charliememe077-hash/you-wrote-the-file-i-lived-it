# What Safeguarding Feels Like Inside the Body

People often talk about safeguarding in professional language.

Thresholds.
Risk.
Assessment.
Presentation.
Compliance.
Behaviour.
Escalation.
Concerns.

But very few people talk about what safeguarding can feel like inside the human body when you are the person living through it.

Especially if you already have trauma.

Especially if you are autistic.
Especially if your nervous system already struggles to feel safe.

I do not think people always understand what prolonged fear does to the body.

You can be trying your absolute hardest to cooperate while internally feeling terrified of being misunderstood.

You can attend every meeting while going home shaking afterwards.

You can answer questions while your nervous system feels like danger is sitting in the room beside you.

You can appear “highly emotional” while actually experiencing survival responses your body cannot simply switch off.

One of the hardest things to explain is that distress is not always danger.

Fear can look intense.
Trauma can look intense.
Autistic overwhelm can look intense.

But intensity alone does not automatically mean somebody is unsafe.

Sometimes it means their nervous system has been carrying fear for too long.

I think many systems underestimate how deeply being observed, assessed, discussed and recorded can affect somebody psychologically when they already live in survival mode.

Especially mothers.

Especially survivors.

Especially people who already spent years learning that safety can disappear very quickly.

Files often record behaviour.

But they do not always record the human body underneath it.
