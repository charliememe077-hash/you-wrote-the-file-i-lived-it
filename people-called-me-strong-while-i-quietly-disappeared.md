# People Called Me Strong While I Quietly Disappeared

People kept calling me strong.

I know they meant well.

But sometimes being called strong felt painful because nobody noticed I was disappearing underneath the survival.

Nobody noticed how exhausted my nervous system had become.

Nobody noticed how long my body had been carrying fear.

People saw me continue.
Continue parenting.
Continue functioning.
Continue surviving.
Continue showing up.

So they assumed I was coping.

But survival and safety are not the same thing.

Many traumatised people become extremely good at functioning while internally overwhelmed.

Especially women.
Especially mothers.
Especially autistic people who learned early that emotions had to be hidden to remain accepted.

I became good at masking distress.

Good at smiling while frightened.
Good at sounding calm while emotionally flooded.
Good at appearing capable while internally exhausted.

And because I could still “function”, people often underestimated how much pressure my nervous system was actually under.

That is one of the loneliest parts of prolonged trauma.

The outside version of you can look far more stable than the reality your body is living internally.

People praise your strength while never realising strength was survival.

Not peace.

Not safety.

Not rest.

Just survival repeated for so long that eventually people mistake it for personality.

I think many traumatised people are carrying invisible exhaustion that the world never fully sees.

And I think being constantly described as “strong” can sometimes hide the fact that somebody desperately needed gentleness, safety and emotional support long before they reached breaking point.
