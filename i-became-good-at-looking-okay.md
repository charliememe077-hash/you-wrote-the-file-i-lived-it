# I Became Good at Looking Okay

That is the part people often misunderstand about trauma.

You do not always fall apart visibly.

Sometimes you become extremely skilled at appearing okay while quietly struggling underneath everything.

I became good at smiling while exhausted.
Good at replying “I’m fine” automatically.
Good at continuing conversations while emotionally overloaded.
Good at functioning while frightened.
Good at carrying pain privately so other people would not feel uncomfortable around it.

And after enough years, even I started believing the performance.

That is what survival mode does.

It teaches you to prioritise functioning over feeling.

Especially if you learned early that vulnerability was unsafe, misunderstood or inconvenient to other people.

I think many traumatised people become experts at emotional masking.

Not because they are dishonest.

Because survival often depends on appearing manageable enough to avoid further emotional harm.

So you keep going.
Keep coping.
Keep functioning.
Keep appearing “strong.”

Even while your nervous system is exhausted underneath it all.

The strange thing is that people often praise you most during the periods you are struggling hardest internally.

Because externally you still look capable.

Still productive.
Still presentable.
Still functioning.

Nobody sees the emotional cost of maintaining that appearance every day.

Nobody sees how much energy survival mode consumes internally.

Especially when autism already means your nervous system is processing sensory, emotional and social information intensely all the time.

I think some of us became so focused on appearing okay that we lost connection with how overwhelmed we actually were.

Until eventually the body forces you to notice.

Through exhaustion.
Shutdown.
Burnout.
Emotional flooding.
Hypervigilance.
Fear.
Physical symptoms.

The body eventually tells the truth survival mode tried to hide.

And sometimes the deepest grief is realising how long you spent performing “okay” while quietly carrying far more pain than anyone around you truly understood.
